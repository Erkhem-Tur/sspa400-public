Book 4 is a Level I Language Laboratory Activities workbook designed for military students learning American English. It accompanies audio recordings and covers four progressive lessons on daily life, military culture, modals, and shopping — all within a US Armed Forces context.

Lesson 1 — Sports, Daily Routines & Past Tense

Introduces sports vocabulary (football, soccer, basketball, tennis, baseball), leisure activities, and city/town life. Grammar focus: regular past tense formation (-ed endings, three pronunciations: /d/, /t/, /ɪd/).

Lesson 2 — Military Life & Irregular Past Tense

Covers military vocabulary: ranks, uniforms, ID cards, saluting, military time (24-hour clock), barracks life, and classroom routines. Grammar: 27 key irregular verbs (go/went, eat/ate, fly/flew, etc.).

Lesson 3 — Clothing, Ability & Permission

Clothing vocabulary (uniform vs. civilian dress). Modal verbs: CAN (ability), MAY (permission), MUST / MUST NOT (obligation and prohibition). Military context dialogs.

Lesson 4 — Questions, Colors & Shopping

WHAT questions with nouns, demonstratives (this/that/these/those), colors, shopping dialogs with HOW MUCH…?, and seasonal vocabulary. Culminates in a shopping role-play at the BX (Base Exchange).

Regular Past Tense — -ed

Add -ed to base verb. Three pronunciations based on final consonant.

/d/ lived · played · answered
/t/ worked · cooked · asked
/ɪd/ wanted · ended · visited

Irregular Past Tense

27 verbs with unpredictable past forms. Must be memorized.

go→went · eat→ate · fly→flew
see→saw · buy→bought · sleep→slept
drive→drove · know→knew

Past Tense Questions

Use DID + subject + base verb for yes/no questions.

Did she cook dinner? Yes, she did.
Did Carl play football? No, he didn't.
He played soccer instead.

Modal: CAN / CANNOT

Expresses ability. Use base verb after CAN. Negative: cannot / can't.

He can fly planes.
She can't hear the tape.
They can speak Spanish.

Modal: MAY / MAY NOT

Expresses permission (more formal than CAN for requests).

May I come in? Yes, you may.
You may write with a pencil.
You may not write with a pen.

Modal: MUST / MUST NOT

Expresses obligation or strong prohibition. Military context.

You must sign in at 0800.
You must not wear civilian clothes.
Students must not come late.

Demonstratives + Noun

This/these (near) vs. that/those (far) + noun.

This coffee is hot.
That coffee is cold.
These gloves are Jane's.

HOW MUCH…?

Asking about price. Works with cost, be, and did.

How much does it cost?
How much is this dress?
How much did it cost before?

WHAT + Noun Questions

What + noun (not just What) to narrow the question.

What language are you learning?
What sports do the students play?
What uniform is he wearing?
